What We Handle

Suspension reasons we build POAs for

If your Seller Performance notice cites any of these, you're in the right place. Each category has a different evidence standard and a different POA structure — we build to the specific one.

Section 3

Violation of Amazon's Seller Code of Conduct

The broadest and most common suspension category. Covers manipulation, account linking, review abuse, and "otherwise violating Section 3." Requires a POA that identifies what Amazon thinks you did — not just what you actually did.

Inauthentic

Inauthentic product or listing complaints

Customer or Amazon rights-holder complaint that the product doesn't match the listing or isn't legitimate. Requires authentic purchase documentation — invoices from manufacturer or authorized distributor, quantities matching your sales.

IP Complaint

Intellectual property infringement

Notice of rights-owner complaint for copyright, trademark, patent, or counterfeit. POA addresses both the retraction path (contact the complainant) and the Amazon-facing defense (why the complaint is resolved or inaccurate).

Safety

Product safety or condition complaints

Customer reports of defective, damaged, expired, or used-sold-as-new product. POA addresses the specific complaint cluster, corrective action on affected inventory, and sourcing or QC changes going forward.

Review Abuse

Review manipulation or fake-review allegations

One of Amazon's fastest-growing enforcement categories. POA addresses whether any review solicitation was compliant, identifies the specific reviews or behaviors flagged, and lays out the operational changes Amazon wants to see.

Account Health

Order Defect Rate or performance-metric suspensions

Suspension driven by ODR, cancellation rate, late shipment rate, or pre-fulfillment cancel rate breaching threshold. POA ties the metric breach to a specific operational root cause and shows the fix.

The POA Structure

Amazon reads three things. In this order.

Seller Performance doesn't read your POA like a letter. They scan it for specific answers. If your POA doesn't answer all three clearly — with evidence where Amazon expects evidence — it gets rejected and you burn your best shot.

01
Root Cause

Why did the issue happen?

Not "we apologize" or "we didn't mean to." Amazon wants a concrete operational or procedural cause — the specific supplier that shipped mixed lot, the listing page detail that was misleading, the inventory process that failed QC. Root cause is not emotional; it's diagnostic.

02
Corrective Actions

What have you already done to fix it?

Concrete, past-tense, ideally verifiable. Removed affected ASINs, contacted the complaining rights-holder, destroyed questionable inventory, refunded affected buyers, switched suppliers. Each action ties to the root cause. Vague promises get rejected.

03
Preventive Measures

How will you keep it from happening again?

This is where most POAs fall apart. "We will be more careful" is not a preventive measure. Amazon wants system-level changes: new supplier vetting process, new listing-QC checklist, new returns-analysis cadence, specific software you've added, specific staff role you've created.
